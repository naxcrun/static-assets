Introduction: Transformative Brotherhood
The Tailor Who Changed the World

During the Nazi occupation of Poland in World War II, the Catholic Church was under severe attack. Priests were sent to concentration camps, public expressions of faith were outlawed, and the culture was being aggressively indoctrinated with a secular, anti-Christian ideology.

In the midst of this darkness lived Jan Tyranowski. Jan wasn't a priest, a theologian, or a powerful politician. He was just a tailor. But at the risk of his own life, Jan opened his apartment to a few young men. He didn't just "hang out" with them; he was highly intentional. He trained them in the spiritual life, taught them how to root out sin, showed them how to pray, and equipped them to go out and lead their peers.

Because of Jan’s intentional discipleship, those young men remained faithful. One of them was a young college student named Karol Wojtyła—the man who would later become Pope St. John Paul II. Jan Tyranowski changed the world because he understood that transformative discipleship happens through intentional, life-on-life investment.

The Reality of the College Campus

While we aren't facing the life-or-death stakes of 1940s Poland, the men of Phi Kappa Theta are stepping onto a Purdue University campus that is bombarded with a secular outlook on life. Everywhere we turn—in the media, in the classroom, and especially in the Greek system—we are handed a distorted view of what it means to be a man, what brings true happiness, and what love actually is.

If we want to build authentic Catholic gentlemen, it is not enough to simply wear the letters, host a few philanthropy events, or show up to Mass on Sunday out of habit. Like Jan Tyranowski, our investment in our brothers must be intentional. We must equip each other to survive the culture and transform it.

The Vision of This Program

This Spiritual Development Program is not just a series of articles to read or a checklist to complete. It is a roadmap for Spiritual Multiplication. It is designed to help you form yourselves and your brothers into missionary disciples who can lead others.

Ultimately, we must remember that Jesus is the One who makes disciples. Any younger brother you lead is technically not your disciple, but a disciple of Christ. Whether you are a senior who is steps ahead in your faith, or a freshman just figuring it out, we are all on the journey together to imitate the Master.

How to Use This Program: The 4 Steps

To maximize the impact of this program within the chapter, every leader should commit to these four steps before sitting down with their brothers:

Preparation: Read and pray through the session’s material on your own. You cannot lead a brother to a place you haven't been yourself. Speak from your own encounter with Christ.

Master the Concepts: Don't just read the text; understand the "Transferable Concepts." What are the core truths of this session that the guys must grasp to survive on campus?

Keep it Personal: This isn't a lecture hall; it's a fraternity. Use the sessions as a tool to build authentic, conversational brotherhood. Share your own struggles and victories.

Demand a Next Step: Discipleship requires action. Never end a session or a 1-on-1 coffee meeting without a concrete takeaway or challenge (like the 30-Day Prayer Challenge or going to Confession).

If done well, with the right vision and with great care, you can change the trajectory of your brothers' lives—and by extension, the culture of Purdue University.

Session 1: Following the Master
Session Goal: To redefine what it means to be a "disciple" from a passive Sunday obligation to an active, daily pursuit of Christ, and to give the brothers a practical tool (Spiritual Breathing) to build a daily prayer habit at Purdue. Estimated Time: 45–60 Minutes Materials Needed: Bibles (or phones with a Bible app), links to the FOCUS articles ("In the Dust of the Rabbi" and "Spiritual Breathing") sent to the group chat beforehand, and printed copies of the 30-Day Prayer Challenge.


1. Welcome & Brotherhood Check-In (10 Minutes)

Start by building the brotherhood. If guys are stressed about midterms or fraternity events, you need to know where their heads are at before diving into the spiritual stuff.

Open with a brief prayer: Ask the Holy Spirit to guide the conversation and bless the chapter.

Highs & Lows: Go around the circle. Every brother shares one "High" (something great that happened this week) and one "Low" (a struggle, stressor, or difficult moment).

Leader Note: You go first to set the tone. Be honest about your "Low"—vulnerability breeds vulnerability.

2. Discussion Part 1: "In the Dust of the Rabbi" (15 Minutes)

The goal here is to help them understand that being a disciple means imitating the master, not just knowing facts about him.

Read aloud to the group:

"In Jesus’ time, a rabbi wasn't just a teacher; he was a master. When a young man was invited to follow a rabbi, his goal wasn't just to learn what the rabbi knew, but to become exactly like him. They followed so closely behind their rabbi on the dirt roads that they would literally be covered in the dust kicked up by his feet."

Ask the Group (Let them do the talking):

When you hear the word "disciple," what usually comes to mind? How does the "dust of the rabbi" image change that perspective?

Think about our fraternity culture and Purdue campus life. What are the "rabbis" or influences that guys on this campus are currently following closely enough to be "covered in their dust"? (e.g., hustle culture, party culture, social media).

What does it practically look like to follow Jesus closely on a Tuesday night at the Phi Kap house?

Leader’s Key Takeaway: Summarize by telling them that being a Catholic gentleman in Phi Kappa Theta means choosing Jesus as our primary Rabbi. It means letting His habits, His virtues, and His way of loving others rub off on us.

3. Discussion Part 2: "Spiritual Breathing" (15 Minutes)

Transitioning from the "what" to the "how." How do we stay close to the Rabbi? Through constant prayer.

Explain the Concept:

Tell the guys: "If following the Rabbi takes energy, prayer is the oxygen. But a lot of us suffocate spiritually in college because we forget how to breathe."

Exhaling (Repentance): Breathing out the bad. This is confessing our sins, acknowledging where we messed up today, and letting go of the stress, pride, and garbage the world throws at us.

Inhaling (Faith & Grace): Breathing in the good. Asking the Holy Spirit to fill you, receiving His forgiveness, and drawing strength from Him.

Ask the Group:

Why is it so easy to "hold our spiritual breath" (forget to pray) while living at Purdue?

Which is harder for you right now: exhaling (admitting your faults and letting go of control) or inhaling (actually making time to sit in silence and receive from God)?

How can we use "Spiritual Breathing" in real-time? (e.g., walking to class, before a tough exam, or when facing a temptation at a party).

4. The Fraternity Challenge: 30 Days of Prayer (10 Minutes)

Don't end the session without a concrete action step. Discipleship requires action.

The Pitch: "Guys, we can talk about being Catholic gentlemen all day, but if we aren't praying, it's just talk. For the next 30 days, as a pod, we are committing to 15 minutes of personal mental prayer every single day."

The Plan:

Pick a Time: Have every guy pull out his phone right now and set a daily alarm. (First thing in the morning, or stopping by St. Tom's between classes, is usually best).

The Format (A.R.R.R.): Suggest a simple way to pray for those 15 minutes.

Acknowledge: What am I feeling? (Stress, joy, anger).

Relate: Tell it to God honestly. Don't filter it.

Receive: Spend 5 minutes in total silence just listening.

Respond: What is God asking me to do today?

Accountability: Tell them you will text the group chat every evening for the next week asking for a "thumbs up" if they got their 15 minutes in. No shame if they missed, but the expectation is honesty.

5. Closing Prayer (5 Minutes)

Ask if anyone has specific prayer intentions for the week (an upcoming exam, family issues, etc.).

Pray a Hail Mary together for the men of the chapter, asking our Lady to help you all stay close to Christ this week.


 

Session 2: The Core of the Chapter - Virtuous Friendship
Session Goal: To help the brothers identify the difference between friendships based on convenience or partying, and true spiritual brotherhood—and to challenge them to build the latter within Phi Kappa Theta. Estimated Time: 45–60 Minutes Materials Needed: Bibles (or phones), link to the FOCUS article ("Fellowship: Virtuous Friendships") sent to the group chat, and a whiteboard or piece of paper to draw out the concepts.


1. Welcome & Accountability Check-In (10 Minutes)

Start by following up on last week. Discipleship requires accountability. If you assigned a challenge last week and don't ask about it, they will learn your challenges don't matter.

Highs & Lows: Go around the circle. Every brother shares one High and one Low from the week.

The Follow-Up: Ask the group directly: "Last week we committed to the 30-Day Prayer Challenge (15 minutes a day). Give me a 1-to-10 on how you did."

Leader Note: If guys failed, don't shame them, but don't let them off the hook either. Ask: “What got in the way? Did you forget, or were you just scrolling TikTok in bed instead? Let’s re-commit for this week.”

2. Discussion Part 1: The Three Levels of Friendship (15 Minutes)

The FOCUS article draws on Aristotle and St. Thomas Aquinas, who taught that there are three types of friendship. You need to break these down so the guys can evaluate their own relationships.

Draw or explain the three levels to the group:

Friendship of Utility: Based on mutual benefit. Examples at Purdue: Your lab partner in a group project, a guy you study with at WALC because he has the old exams, or a networking connection. Once the class ends, the friendship usually ends.

Friendship of Pleasure: Based on shared fun and good times. Examples at Purdue: Your intramural basketball teammates, guys you play Xbox with, or your drinking buddies at Harry's or a chapter tailgate. It’s fun, but it’s entirely based on the activity.

Friendship of Virtue: Based on a mutual pursuit of the good. You aren't just using them, and you aren't just having fun with them. You actively want them to get to Heaven, and you are willing to sacrifice for them.

Ask the Group (Let them do the talking):

Look at your phone contacts right now. What percentage of your friendships fall into the "Utility" or "Pleasure" buckets?

Why is it so rare to find a true "Friendship of Virtue" on a college campus today?

3. Discussion Part 2: Fraternal Application (15 Minutes)

This is where you bring it home to the fraternity house. Greek life is incredible at building Level 2 friendships, but Phi Kappa Theta is called to Level 3.

Read aloud to the group:

"Proverbs 27:17 says, 'Iron sharpens iron, and one man sharpens another.' But sharpening iron creates friction. It creates sparks. True brothers don't just tell you what you want to hear; they tell you what you need to hear."

Ask the Group:

Think about the culture of our chapter. Are we mostly Level 2 (Pleasure) or Level 3 (Virtue)?

What does it look like when a friendship moves from Pleasure to Virtue? (e.g., Being willing to call a brother out if he's drinking too much, disrespecting women, or skipping Mass).

Have you ever had a friend who made you a better man just by being around them? What were they like?

Leader’s Key Takeaway: Summarize by telling them that the letters on their chest mean nothing if they are perfectly fine watching their brothers live in a state of mortal sin. True brotherhood means looking out for each other's souls, not just each other's social lives.

4. The Fraternity Challenge: The "Go Deep" Conversation (10 Minutes)

Move them from theory to practice.

The Pitch: "Guys, we can't have Level 3 friendships with all 60+ guys in the chapter. Jesus had 12 apostles, but He only had 3 (Peter, James, and John) that He took with Him to the most intense moments. You need a core group of guys who have your back spiritually."

The Plan:

Identify the Target: Ask each guy to mentally pick one brother in the chapter (or in this pod) with whom they currently have a "Pleasure" friendship, but want to build a "Virtue" friendship.

The Action: Sometime this week, invite that brother to grab lunch at Ford or a coffee at Greyhouse.

The Rule: During that meal, they must ask one question that goes deeper than school, sports, or weekend plans.

Examples: "How are you actually doing right now?" / "What's the most stressful thing in your life right now?" / "Where are you at with your faith these days?"

5. Closing Prayer (5 Minutes)

Intentions: Take prayer requests.

Closing: Pray the Our Father together. Ask God to forge bonds of authentic virtue within the chapter, and pray for the courage to be the one who initiates those deeper conversations.

 


 

Session 3: Character & Accountability
Session Goal: To redefine "freedom," introduce the concept of Moral Authority, and challenge the brothers to live out "The Big 3" (Chastity, Sobriety, and Excellence) through mutual accountability. Estimated Time: 45–60 Minutes Materials Needed: Phones/Bibles, links to the FOCUS articles ("Free to Love" and "Moral Authority"), and printed copies of the Virtue Plan for each brother.


1. Welcome & Accountability Check-In (10 Minutes)

Brotherhood is built on trust and follow-through. Hold their feet to the fire on last week's challenge.

Highs & Lows: Quick lap around the circle. One High, one Low from the week.

The Follow-Up: Last week, the challenge was to have a "Go Deep" conversation—to grab a meal or coffee with a brother and ask a real, Level 3 (Virtue) friendship question.

Ask: "Who actually did it? How did it go? Was it awkward, or was the guy relieved you actually asked about his life?"

Check-in on Prayer: "Are we still hitting our 15 minutes of daily prayer, or did midterms derail us? Let's reset today."

2. Discussion Part 1: True Freedom & "The Big 3" (15 Minutes)

The world's definition of freedom is completely backwards from the Catholic definition. You need to untangle this for them.

Explain the Concept of Freedom:

The World’s Definition: Freedom is the ability to do whatever I want, whenever I want, with whoever I want, with no consequences.

The Catholic Definition: Freedom is the ability to choose the good. If you "choose" to drink every weekend but literally cannot stop yourself, you aren't free; you are a slave to alcohol. If you "choose" to look at porn but can't go a week without it, you are a slave to your screen. True freedom is having control over your own intellect and will.

Introduce "The Big 3" (from the Free to Love article): If we want to be free, and if we want the Moral Authority to lead this fraternity, we have to master three areas:

Sobriety: Not necessarily total abstinence (if you are of age), but never losing your intellect or will to intoxication. It means knowing your limit and stopping.

Chastity: Viewing women as sisters in Christ, not objects for consumption. This means rejecting the hookup culture, fighting the battle against pornography, and honoring the women on Purdue's campus.

Excellence in Daily Duty: You are at Purdue to be a student. If you skip class, cheat on exams, and sleep until 2:00 PM, you cannot be a spiritual leader. Holiness starts with doing your actual job well.

Ask the Group (Let them do the talking):

Which definition of freedom is actively promoted in the Greek system here at Purdue?

Looking at "The Big 3," which one is the hardest to live out while living in a fraternity house?

3. Discussion Part 2: Moral Authority (15 Minutes)

Transition to how their personal character impacts the chapter.

Read aloud to the group:

"Moral Authority is the weight your words carry because of the way you live your life. You can hold a title on the Exec Board, but if your brothers see you completely compromised on the weekends, you have zero Moral Authority when you try to lead them on a Tuesday."

Ask the Group:

Have you ever had a coach, teacher, or older brother whose words carried massive weight? Why did you respect them?

If a guy in our chapter is constantly blacking out at functions or treating girls poorly, how does that impact the reputation of Phi Kappa Theta as a whole?

Leader Vulnerability: Share a time you lost Moral Authority because of a bad choice, or a time you had to make a hard choice to keep it.

Leader’s Key Takeaway: Summarize by telling them that leadership isn't about giving great speeches at chapter meetings. It's about living a life that demands respect. If they want to elevate the men around them, they have to clean up their own lives first.

4. The Fraternity Challenge: The Virtue Plan (10 Minutes)

We don't conquer sin by accident. We need a plan and a brother to watch our six.

The Pitch: "Guys, the devil thrives in secrecy. If you are fighting a battle in the dark, you will lose. Today, we are bringing it into the light. We are going to build a Virtue Plan."

The Plan:

Hand out the printed Virtue Plans (or have them pull out the notes app on their phones).

Identify the Root: Ask each guy to silently identify the one area of "The Big 3" where he is struggling the most right now.

The Strategy: What is one practical boundary you can set this week? (e.g., "I will put my phone across the room before I go to sleep to avoid porn," or "I will switch to water after two beers at the tailgate.")

The Brotherhood Pact: Have them pair up with the guy sitting next to them. This is their accountability partner for the week. They give that brother explicit permission to text them on Friday night and ask, "Are you sticking to the plan?"

5. Closing Prayer (5 Minutes)

Intentions: Take specific prayer requests.

Closing: Pray the Prayer to St. Michael the Archangel together. Remind the guys that this is a spiritual battle for their character, and St. Michael is the perfect patron for men fighting for virtue.


 

Session 4: The Sacraments
Session Goal: To help the brothers realize that living as a Catholic gentleman cannot be done on pure willpower; it requires the supernatural fuel of the Eucharist and the healing grace of Confession. Estimated Time: 45–60 Minutes Materials Needed: Phones/Bibles, links to the FOCUS articles ("The Eucharist" and "The Healing Power of Confession") sent to the group chat.


1. Welcome & Accountability Check-In (10 Minutes)

Last week was heavy. You asked them to pick a major area of struggle and make a plan. You must follow up, or the Virtue Plan becomes just another piece of paper.

Highs & Lows: Go around the circle. One High, one Low from the week.

The Follow-Up: Ask the group directly: "Last week we built our Virtue Plans and partnered up. Who actually texted their accountability partner this weekend? Did anyone have a moment where that plan kept them from making a mistake?"

Leader Note: Acknowledge that fighting for virtue is exhausting. If guys failed their Virtue Plan this week, this is the perfect transition into today’s topic: we need supernatural help.

2. Discussion Part 1: Willpower vs. Grace (15 Minutes)

We live in a "grind" culture that tells us we can fix ourselves if we just work harder. In the spiritual life, that leads to burnout and despair.

Explain the Concept:

The Willpower Trap: When we try to beat our sins (like impurity, pride, or drunkenness) using only our own willpower, we eventually hit a wall. Willpower is like a battery; it drains when you are tired, stressed about a Purdue exam, or lonely.

The Sacramental Solution: The Sacraments aren't just empty rituals or Catholic "chores." They are direct encounters with Jesus where He actually injects His own strength (Grace) into our weak spiritual batteries.

Ask the Group (Let them do the talking):

Read John 6:53-56 out loud. Jesus says His flesh is true food and His blood is true drink. Why is it so easy for us to treat Sunday Mass as a box to check rather than the literal fuel we need to survive the week?

Think about the guys in the chapter who seem the most grounded and at peace. Do you notice a correlation between their peace and how often they go to Mass or Adoration at St. Tom's?

3. Discussion Part 2: The Hospital for Sinners (15 Minutes)

Fraternity culture often encourages guys to boast about their sins or hide their shame. Confession destroys both pride and shame.

Read aloud to the group:

"The Church is not a museum for saints; it's a hospital for sinners. When we fall, the devil tries to convince us to hide—just like Adam and Eve did in the garden. Confession is where we stop hiding, step into the light, and let the Great Physician actually heal the wound."

Ask the Group:

Why is going to Confession so intimidating, especially for college guys? (Discuss the fear of being judged by the priest, the shame of saying sins out loud, etc.).

Leader Vulnerability: Share a brief story about a time you went to Confession after carrying a heavy burden of sin or guilt, and describe the physical and spiritual relief you felt walking out of the church.

How would the culture of Phi Kappa Theta change if it became completely normal for brothers to say to each other, "Hey, I messed up this week, I need to go to Confession. You want to walk over with me?"

Leader’s Key Takeaway: Summarize by telling them that the strongest men in the fraternity are not the ones who pretend they are perfect. The strongest men are the ones who have the humility to drop to their knees, admit they failed, and let Christ pick them back up.

4. The Fraternity Challenge: The Sacramental Strike (10 Minutes)

You cannot just talk about the Sacraments; you have to go receive them.

The Pitch: "Guys, if we are starving, talking about a steak dinner doesn't give us any calories. We actually have to eat. This week, we are going to the source together."

The Plan:

The Mass Pact: Look at the weekend schedule for St. Tom's right now. Agree on one specific Mass time (e.g., Sunday at 7:00 PM or 9:00 PM) that this pod will attend together.

The Confession Challenge: Find out when Confessions are offered at St. Tom's this week. Challenge every guy in the pod to go.

The Wingman System: Tell them: "Nobody likes going to the hospital alone. If you haven't been to Confession in three months or three years, it doesn't matter. Text me, and I will literally walk over to St. Tom's with you and wait in line with you."

5. Closing Prayer (5 Minutes)

Intentions: Take specific prayer requests.

Closing: Pray the Anima Christi (Soul of Christ) or a simple Act of Contrition together. Remind the brothers that God's mercy is infinitely bigger than whatever mistakes they made at the house this past weekend.


 

Session 5: Forming the Catholic Mind
Session Goal: To equip the brothers to intellectually engage with their faith, defend it in a secular environment, and actively feed their minds with Catholic truth rather than just cultural noise. Estimated Time: 45–60 Minutes Materials Needed: Phones/Bibles, links to the FOCUS article ("The Apostles' Teaching: The Battle for Your Mind") sent to the group chat.


1. Welcome & Accountability Check-In (10 Minutes)

Last week's challenge was a big one—going to the Sacraments. This is where you see who is actually buying into the program.

Highs & Lows: Go around the circle. One High, one Low from the week.

The Follow-Up: Ask the group directly: "Last week we committed the 'Sacramental Strike.' Did we actually make it to Mass at St. Tom's together? Did anyone take the step to go to Confession?"

Leader Note: Celebrate the victories. If guys went to Confession for the first time in years, acknowledge how huge that is. If the pod dropped the ball, remind them that grace is always available and challenge them to go this week.

2. Discussion Part 1: The Battle for Your Mind (15 Minutes)

We consume hours of content every day. What we put into our minds inevitably shapes what we believe and how we act.

Explain the Concept:

The Purdue Bubble: You are getting a world-class education right now. You are studying complex calculus, advanced coding, or high-level economics. But for a lot of us, our theological education stopped in 8th-grade Confirmation class.

The Imbalance: When you have a college-level understanding of the world but an 8th-grade understanding of God, your faith will eventually collapse under the weight of secular arguments. Faith and reason are not opposites; they are two wings that elevate the human mind to truth.

Ask the Group (Let them do the talking):

On an average day, how many hours are you feeding your mind with secular media (TikTok, YouTube, Netflix, music) versus Catholic truth?

Have you ever had a professor or a classmate at Purdue openly mock Christianity or Catholic moral teaching? How did you respond (or why didn't you respond)?

Why is it dangerous to rely only on our feelings or emotions to sustain our faith, rather than understanding the actual logic and theology behind it?

3. Discussion Part 2: Debates in the Chapter House (15 Minutes)

Fraternity houses are prime spots for late-night debates. Your brothers need to know how to handle these conversations with truth and charity.

Read aloud to the group:

"1 Peter 3:15 says, 'Always be prepared to make a defense to any one who calls you to account for the hope that is in you, yet do it with gentleness and reverence.' When we don't know our faith, we usually react to challenges with anger, or we just awkwardly agree with the culture to avoid conflict."

Ask the Group:

Think about the late-night conversations that happen in the Phi Kap house or at parties. When topics like abortion, the hookup culture, or the existence of God come up, do you feel equipped to explain why the Church teaches what she does?

What is the difference between winning an argument to prove someone wrong, and sharing the truth to win a brother's soul?

Leader Vulnerability: Share a time you were backed into a corner about your faith and didn't know the answer, and what you did about it.

Leader’s Key Takeaway: Summarize by telling them that ignorance is a choice. We have 2,000 years of the greatest philosophers, scientists, and theologians backing up the Catholic faith. If you don't know the answer to a tough question, the right response is: "That's a great question. I don't know the exact answer, but let me research it and get back to you."

4. The Fraternity Challenge: The "Mind Feed" (10 Minutes)

We need to actively replace the cultural noise with Catholic truth.

The Pitch: "Guys, if you eat garbage every day, you're going to feel terrible. The same goes for your mind. This week, we are going to upgrade our intellectual diet."

The Plan:

The Content Swap: Challenge every brother to swap out just 15–20 minutes of their daily media consumption (e.g., listening to music on the walk to class, or scrolling before bed) with high-quality Catholic content.

Provide Options: Give them a quick menu right now and have them subscribe on their phones:

Podcasts: "Pints with Aquinas," "Catholic Stuff You Should Know," or FOCUS's "The SEEK Podcast."

YouTube: Bishop Robert Barron / Word on Fire, Ascension Presents (Fr. Mike Schmitz).

Reading: Start reading a classic like C.S. Lewis's Mere Christianity or a book by a saint.

The Pod Check-In: Next week, every guy has to bring one cool fact, argument, or perspective they learned from the Catholic content they consumed.

5. Closing Prayer (5 Minutes)

Intentions: Take specific prayer requests, especially for any tough midterms or projects coming up.

(e.g., Serving breakfast at the Lafayette Transitional Housing Center, or going out of your way to help an elderly neighbor near the chapter house). Tell them to bring their "Timothys" with them.

5. Closing Prayer (5 Minutes)

Intentions: Have the brothers verbally call out the first names of the guys on their "Hit List."

Closing: Pray the Memorare together. Ask the Blessed Mother to intercede for the men in the chapter who are currently furthest away from Christ, and pray that the brothers in this room have the grit to lead them back.


 

Session 6: The Burden of Leadership
Program Stage: Equip — Semester 4: The Guide

Session Goal: To prepare the brothers for the inevitable pushback, failure, and burnout that comes with spiritual leadership, and to teach them how to unite those frustrations to the Cross of Christ. Estimated Time: 45–60 Minutes Materials Needed: Phones/Bibles, links to the FOCUS articles ("Pursuing Christlike Character" and "Take up your Cross and Follow Me").


1. Welcome & Accountability Check-In (10 Minutes)

Leadership requires endurance. Check the pulse on their spiritual sacrifices from last week.

Highs & Lows: Go around the circle. One High, one Low from the week.

The Follow-Up: Last week, the challenge was to offer up one specific sacrifice (fasting, cold shower, extra prayer time) for the "Timothys" they are mentoring.

Ask: "Who actually did it? When you felt the discomfort of skipping coffee or taking that cold shower, did it change the way you looked at the guy you were praying for?"

Check on the Service Project: "Did we lock in a date to go serve at the Lafayette Transitional Housing Center or a local charity?"

2. Discussion Part 1: The Reality of Pushback (15 Minutes)

You need to normalize failure. If they think a perfect leader never gets rejected, they will quit the first time a younger guy laughs at their invitation.

Explain the Concept:

The Honeymoon is Over: When you first decide to step up and lead this chapter spiritually, it feels exciting. But eventually, reality hits. Guys will skip your Bible study because they are hungover. Guys you poured hours into will make terrible decisions on a Saturday night. Some brothers might even actively mock you for taking your faith so seriously.

The Trap of Resentment: When this happens, the easiest reaction is pride. You start thinking, "I'm working so hard for these guys, and they don't care. Forget them." That is exactly what the enemy wants.

Ask the Group (Let them do the talking):

What is the most frustrating part of trying to lead guys in this chapter right now?

Read John 15:18-20 aloud: "If the world hates you, know that it has hated me before it hated you... A servant is not greater than his master." Why are we so surprised when trying to live like a Catholic gentleman at Purdue actually costs us social capital?

3. Discussion Part 2: Embracing the Cross on Mission (15 Minutes)

We don't just endure suffering; we use it. This is the core of Catholic theology.

Read aloud to the group:

"If our goal is to imitate Jesus, we have to remember where His mission ended. It didn't end with Him being universally loved by everyone; it ended on a Cross. The frustrations, the late nights dealing with chapter drama, and the sting of rejection are not signs that you are failing as a leader. They are the exact tools God is using to carve Christlike character into your soul."

Ask the Group:

Think about the concept of "Redemptive Suffering." When a younger brother breaks your heart by making a destructive choice, how can you offer that emotional pain up to God as a prayer for his conversion, rather than just getting angry?

How does embracing the heavy, unglamorous work of leadership actually make us better future husbands and fathers?

Leader Vulnerability: Share a moment from your time in the chapter where you felt completely burned out or defeated as a leader, and how you had to bring that directly to God.

4. The Fraternity Challenge: The Leadership Examen (10 Minutes)

To survive the long haul into their senior year, they need a daily tool to process the heavy burden of leadership.

The Pitch: "Guys, if you carry the weight of this chapter's culture on your own shoulders, it will crush you. You have to hand it back to God every single night. We are going to adopt the daily Examen."

The Plan:

The Tool: Explain the 5 simple steps of the Ignatian Examen (Pray for Light, Give Thanks, Review the Day, Repent, Resolve for Tomorrow).

The Leadership Twist: Challenge them to run through this specifically with their "Timothys" in mind.

Give Thanks: Name one good thing you saw in a younger brother today.

Review: Where did I fail to be a good example today? Was I too harsh with a guy? Was I a coward when I should have spoken up?

Resolve: Give tomorrow's interactions back to Christ.

The Commitment: Challenge every man in the pod to run through this 5-minute Examen while lying in bed tonight before they fall asleep.

5. Closing Prayer & Commissioning (5 Minutes)

The Final Charge: Look the guys in the eye. Tell them: "Phase 2 is over. The formal training wheels are off. You are now the spiritual vanguard of Phi Kappa Theta. The younger guys are watching your every move. Lead them well."

Closing: Pray the Anima Christi or the St. Michael Prayer together. Ask the Holy Spirit to give these men the endurance to carry the cross of leadership all the way through to graduation.
